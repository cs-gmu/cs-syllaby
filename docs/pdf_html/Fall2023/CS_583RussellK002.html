2. Course Overview

2.1 Prerequisite Knowledge (click to expand)

  • What classes do I need to have taken? You will be using knowledge introduced in a Formal Methods and Models course (such as CS 330) and by extension Discrete Mathematics (in Math 125). You also need knowledge gained from a Data Structures course (such as CS 310) and by extension multiple programming courses (such as CS 112 and CS 211). An undergraduate algorithms course is also helpful, but not required.
  • What specific information from those classes do I need? Below is a list of many (but not all) topics which may be "coming back to haunt you":
    • Programming - Programming Logic, Recursion, Searching, Sorting, Big-O Analysis
    • Discrete Math - Proof Techniques, Mathematical Induction, Sets, Graphs, Trees, Recursion, Enumeration
    • Formal Methods and Models - Logic, Proofs, Mathematical Induction, Program Verification, Computability
    • Data Structures - Big-O Analysis, Lists, Stacks, Queues, Sets and Maps, Hash Tables, Trees, Search Trees, Priority Queues, Heaps, Graphs

2.2 Course Topics (click to expand)

  • What is in this course? Topics include analyzing sequential and parallel algorithmic strategies such as greedy methods, divide and conquer strategies, dynamic programming, search and traversal techniques, and approximation algorithms; and analyzing specific algorithms falling into these classes, NP-Hard and NP-Complete problems.
  • What parts of the textbook will we cover? As a general outline, we will be covering the following from the book:
    • Introductory Material (portions of ch 1-5)
    • Analysis Sorting and Order Statistics (ch 5-9)
    • Analysis of Advanced Data Structures (ch 11-13, 17, 19)
    • Design and Advanced Analysis Techniques (ch 14-16)
    • Graph Algorithms (ch 20-25)
    • NP-completeness and Approximation Algorithms (ch 34-35)
    A week-by-week schedule will be posted on Piazza with more details.

3. Semester Grades and Graded Materials

3.1 Semester Grades (click to expand)

  • What things will be graded? You will be doing two main things for grades: doing homework and taking exams. Homework will be weekly. Exams will be a midterm and a final. There is much more extensive information on these materials in the sections below.
  • How much is each task worth?
    The following is a breakdown of the class tasks:

    Category Percent
    Homework 30%
    Midterm Exam 30%
    Final Exam 40%

  • How do semester grades equate to letter grades? We will be using the +/- system in the following way:
    • A+ (>= 98.0%) A (>= 92.0%) A– (>= 90.0%)
    • B+ (>= 88.0%) B (>= 82.0%) B– (>= 80.0%)
    • C (>= 70.0%)
    • F (< 60.0%)
  • Do you round? I do not round individual components (exams, homework, etc.), but I do round the semester grade up to the nearest whole number before determining the letter grade. Please do not ask for more rounding.
  • Do you curve? I do not curve grades, and you should never ask anyone for a curve. A real curve pits students against each other and your grade might go down if there is someone very talented in your class. At its heart, a curve is a promise to fail some percentage of the students in the class, and I'd prefer everyone be able to pass by doing the work, even if everyone else is also doing the work.
  • Ok... then do you curve up? Not for the semester grades, but if I belive there is something "off" about the grades on some particular assignment (based on my knowledge of the class in general, historical information, etc.), I will make appropriate adjustments to that one item. Please understand that all adjustments must be consistent with the grading shown above and fair for all students.
  • Can I do additional work or redo assignments at the end of the semester? There will be no make-up assignments or unannounced extra-credit opportunities at the end of the semester; your grade should be a measure of your semester-long progress.
  • What if I need to pass this class? If you tell me at the start of the semester, we can definitely work together to make sure you're on track and work out any recovery that needs to happen if you start to struggle. Every semester I have one or more students who "check in" with me during office hours because they need the extra support, reassurance, or just some accountability. This is a perfect use for office hours. If you tell me at the end of the semester that you needed to pass but aren't currently passing, then there will be nothing I can do to help!

3.2 Homework(click to expand)

General Homework Questions:

  • What types of homework will there be? You will have paper-and-pencil homework each week that is due in class the next week.
  • Where will I submit homework? Homework must be submitted in person in class unless you have a university excused absence and/or instructor approval.
  • Will you "drop" one or more homework assignments? No. This is a graduate class. No homework can be dropped for any reason (except emergencies, see questions below in "Homework Issues and Potential Problems").
  • Can I work in a group? Can I work with an AI? Homework is considered individual effort unless otherwise authorized by me, and the resources permitted are only those provided by be. Therefore no sharing of solutons (and no discussion of solutions) is allowed with anyone except the TAs and me. So, in short, if the person or thing helping you is not: (a) me, (b) an official TA for the course, (c) my course material -- slides, code, etc., (d) the textbook, or (e) your own brain, you may not use it.

Getting Help on Homework:

  • So how can I get help on homework? Homework help is available in-person before/after class or in office hours, but the discussion board (Piazza) also gives a way to get asynchronous help. Don't spend days banging your head against a wall, come get help!
  • Where and when are office hours? These will be posted on Piazza as either a pinned post or on the "Resources" page, "Staff" tab.
  • Can I discuss my homework with my classmates? Before the homework is returned for grading, no. After the homework is returned, you may discuss your solutions with classmates in your class section this semester. However, please don't give anyone a copy of your homework, even after grading, as you never know how they will use it.
  • How can I get getter at time management so I'm not doing everything at the last minute? Instructors can often help with this, but GMU Learning Services offers a lot of support options for time management improvement as well. Whatever you do, don't hide the problem, talk to someone about it!
  • What can I do to improve my writing skills (so that I don't lose points on homework or exams)? I'd recommend working with the Writing Center. They also have specific resources to assist with English for Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL)

Homework Issues and Potential Problems

  • What if I miss (or will miss) a homework deadline because of some sort of emergency? If you need an extension or miss a due date because of a university-accepted emergency (such as being taken to the hospital), just notify me within 24 hours to make arrangements. If you provide documentation of the emergency and your involvement we will work out something that makes sense for the situation. I may elect to "exempt" a missed homework rather than give an extension under certain circumstances. In this case, other homework in the same sub-category will gain more weight. Unfortunately, to ensure the class moves forward for everyone, failure to follow these policies will result in a zero.
  • What if I miss a homework deadline for some other reason? Missing a homework due date for any non-university-accepted reason (such as not paying attention to when the due date is), will result in a zero.
  • What if I know I will need an extension in advance? Extensions require exceptional circumstances (as judged by me) and must be arranged in advance. I don't give extensions on or after the due day unless you have a university-accepted emergency. Normal obligations such as family or friend events, vacations, or other classes are not exceptional circumstances (unless hospitals are involved!).
  • Can the TAs give me an extension? Nope. Just me. GTAs cannot approve late submissions or extensions.

Classes with Electronic Submissions

  • What if I submit the wrong files electronically? Just like turning in the wrong homework in-person, turning in the wrong homework electronically will result in a zero. Just because it's easy to make a mistake, doesn't mean you shouldn't double check your submission (in fact, it means the opposite!).
  • What if I make a "stupid" mistake on my homework? It happens, but it also has consequences. To prepare you for the cautiousness you need when working in a professional environment, you will not be able to fix anything after submission, even if it is a simple mistake. That's what the time before submission is for. Make sure to dedicate specific time to looking over your work before submission.
  • What if Blackboard or Gradescope is down? In the rare situation that a website/tool/service is somehow unavailable or giving an error, you are still responsible for turning in on time. Just email your submission to me before the deadline and explain what happened.
  • What if my computer crashes and I lose my work? To ensure professional practices in our senior student body, catastrophic computer failure will not be considered a reason for an extension. As this is a senior level CS course, you can practice the professionalism you'll need in the workplace by backing up your work.
  • What type of backup should I use? You can use online services like OneDrive (free to students, see: https://its.gmu.edu/service/office-365-onedrive/), or something as simple as emailing things to yourself at the end of each day, whatever gets the job done!

4. University and Departmental Policies and Resources

4.1 Honor Code (click to expand)

  • What is the GMU Honor Code? The GMU Honor Code describes the behaviors that are not allowed in an academic setting, such as cheating, plagiarism, stealing, and lying. This policy is rigorously enforced. All students must abide by the GMU Honor Code.
  • What is the CS Honor Code? The computer science department has a CS Honor Code that you are also subject to. The document helps you to understand how GMU honor code applies to CS, such as what constitutes cheating in a CS setting, and it clarifies some scenarios that are unique to our sorts of assignments.
  • What is the Honor Court? If you are suspected of violating the GMU or CS Honor Code, you will be referred to the Office of Academic Integrity who will present you with options, such as contesting the charge or admitting the violation. The purpose of the honor court is to hears both sides in the case. This way it is not your professors who get to determine what occurred and professors can't unilaterally take action against you.
  • Is the Honor Court a sentencing court? No. The honor court is there to ensure a fair hearing before punishments are given. The referral to the the Office of Academic Integrity is not a punishment in itself, it is a way of getting the situation clearly understood before any penalties are applied.
  • What happens if I violate the honor code (cheat, plagiarize, steal, or lie)? The CS Department take the honor code quite seriously. Any violations of the honor code simply will not be tolerated. The penalty for cheating will always be far worse than a zero grade, to ensure it's not worth taking the chance. Confirmed cases of cheating almost always translate into course failure. Suspected cheating will be referred to the Honor Committee as outlined in the GMU Honor Code.
  • What happens if someone steals my work? It is your responsibility to lock your computer(s) with a password, to not post your work to websites like Pastebin that are publicly accessible, to guard your USB drives and computers, to not upload your files to someone else's computer, etc. You will be liable for any access gained to your work resulting from poor computer security practices.
  • Can you give me an example of cheating? There are many examples in the honor codes listed above, if you are ever unsure of what would/wouldn't be cheating, just ask!
  • Why can't I do ___? I hear that's what they do in industry. Why is that cheating? If you want to play in a band, you need to know how to play your own instrument. School is like learning to play the instrument, industy is like being in the band. I worked for over a decade in industry, so I know what people do and don't do, come talk to me if you want a much longer discussion of this :)

4.5 Long and Short Term Injuries and Disabilites (click to expand)

  • I got injured and it's impacting my ability to do class tasks and/or homework. What should I do? The Office of Disability Services also handles services for many temporary disabilities (for example, if you break your arm, they can arrange note taking services). Please contact them if you discover any sudden impairment that affects your ability to function in class and/or on your projects. They have a quick and easy registration process. All you need is documentation for your condition.
  • I have an undocumented disability, or other condition that impacts my learning. What should I do? This depends a lot on the nature of your condition, but generally it's best to talk to someone about what's happening. Often instructors can't offer support on an ad-hoc basis, but they can often help you determine if the situation might be helped by some coaching (e.g. minor test anxiety), counseling (e.g. major anxiety), or something you can get temporary support for from the Office of Disability Services (e.g. a broken arm).
  • I have a documented physical or learning disability. What should I do? If you have not yet registered with the Office of Disability Services, you should. This office offers accommodations (such as extended time on projects and/or exams, quite testing environments, lecture recording waivers, etc.) to assist with everything from broken arms to chronic/acute depression and anxiety. I'd highly recommend talking to them if you think any of their services might help. Every student I know who works with them speaks highly of them. They have a quick and easy registration process. All you need is documentation for your condition. Even if you don't know whether you'll need to use the accommodations, it's best get your condition documented with the university as accommodations are not retroactive.
  • I am registered with the Office of Disability Services, but I'm not sure if I want to use the accommodations. What should I do? For all your classes (not just this one), you should still speak with your the instructor to discuss your approved accommodations. Even if you don't know whether you plan on using the accommodations, once an instructor knows you have them, you can easily begin using them if the need arises. It's better to be over prepared than under prepared, since accommodations are not retroactive.
